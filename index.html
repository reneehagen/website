ABOUT ME

I am: a graduate student at UCLA, a data scientist, an aspiring policy analyst and a cycling enthusiast!

A little bit about my background: I started my education with a bachelor's degree in psychology at Leiden University in the Netherlands, where my interest in grew after being exposed to cross-cultural psychology. I soon decided to pursue a master's degree in cultural anthropology and development sociology (also in Leiden) and graduated Cum Laude with my research on a hunter-gatherer population in the Philippines and their first exposure to formal mainstream education. After some years working for non-governmental organizations in the Netherlands and the Philippines I moved to the United States to start a graduate degree in biological anthropology at UCLA. Here I was trained in the field of behavioral ecology, a theoretical approach that uses evolutionary theory to explain and predict how individual-level and group-level socio-ecological factors influence human behavior. At UCLA my research is focused on issues regarding gender inequality related to topics such as maternal health and women’s decreased earnings after having their first child.

RESEARCH

MY RESEARCH INTERESTS

Behavioral ecology

My formal graduate education is in behavioral ecology. This field seeks use evolutionary theory to explain and predict how individual-level and population-wide socio-ecological factors shape people’s behavior. My dissertation research has focused on issues regarding gender inequality-related to topics such as maternal health among women in rural Namibia, women's economic bargaining power and their vulnerability to intimate-partner violence, and women's and men's decrease in earnings after their first child.

Social inequality and policy

More recently I have focused on applying my anthropological background to studying the actual and potential impact of social policies on people’s lives. In one study, I examine how increased access to education in low- and middle-income countries has affected women's bargaining position in marriage and has downstream consequences for the prevalence of intimate-partner violence. As part of another project I am studying the 'child penalty': the earnings parents - especially mothers - lose after having a first child. And, as a fellow at the WORLD Policy Analysis Center I evaluated the impact of disability policies worldwide on the economic situation of people with disabilities.

Statistics

Using the rigorous training in multi-variate regression models I received at UCLA, and I sought out my own education in statistics for the social sciences. There are so many amazing resources that exist for this online! I learned the basics of Bayesian inference through Richard McElreath's online course Statistical Rethinking. I further built on this knowledge using free online sources such as Bayes Rules! by Alicia Johnson, Miles Ott and Mine Dogucu, Andrew Heiss' blog, and Causal Inference: the Mixed tape from Scott Cunningham.

Programming

I strongly identify as an R lady. There are just so many things you can do with R: data cleaning, statistics, data visualization, website building... Other than that I'm also comfortable using SQL for data management, Ubuntu and Unix for working on secure high performance computing servers, and I've built a few simmple Android apps in Dart. Oh, and this is the first website I've built, so you can be the judge of my skills in html ;)
